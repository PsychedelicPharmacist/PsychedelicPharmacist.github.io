Wellness of the Planet

Mon, Nov 11, 2024 – Tue, Nov 11, 2025

← Back to Educational Events

We are part of nature and it is part of us…

As our planet faces unprecedented ecological challenges, psychedelics may offer a profound shift in our relationship with the natural world—rekindling an ancient recognition that human wellness and planetary wellness are inseparable.

The Ecological Crisis and Disconnection

We stand at a critical juncture in human history. Climate change, biodiversity loss, pollution, and resource depletion threaten not only the health of our planetary systems but our own survival as a species. Yet despite mounting scientific evidence and increasingly visible impacts, our collective response remains inadequate.

Many researchers and philosophers have suggested that at the root of our ecological crisis lies a profound disconnection—from nature, from each other, and even from our own deeper selves. This disconnection manifests in exploitative relationships with the natural world, where we view ecosystems primarily as resources to be used rather than living systems of which we are an integral part.

Psychedelics and Nature Connection

A growing body of research suggests that psychedelic experiences can reliably increase feelings of nature-connectedness—the subjective sense of being part of, rather than separate from, the natural world. This effect appears to persist long after the acute experience has ended:

  • A 2019 study found significant increases in nature-relatedness following psychedelic experiences that remained elevated at least two years later
  • Research at Imperial College London has shown that psilocybin-assisted therapy increased nature-connectedness in patients with treatment-resistant depression
  • Naturalistic studies of ayahuasca ceremonies suggest increased environmental awareness and concern among participants

This reconnection to nature appears to be more than just a pleasant side effect. For many, it becomes a profound shift in worldview that informs their values, behaviors, and relationship with the planet.

Indigenous Wisdom and Ecological Knowledge

Indigenous cultures that have worked with psychedelic plant medicines for millennia often maintain worldviews that recognize the interconnectedness of all life and the sacred nature of ecological relationships. These traditions often include sophisticated ecological knowledge and sustainable practices born from generations of intimate relationship with local ecosystems.

From the Shipibo people of the Amazon who work with ayahuasca, to the Mazatec of Mexico who use psilocybin mushrooms in healing ceremonies, many indigenous traditions center a reverence for and reciprocity with the natural world. As psychedelics reenter Western consciousness, there's much we can learn from these traditions, while being mindful of the need to respect cultural sovereignty and avoid appropriation.

Eco-psychological Perspectives

The field of ecopsychology proposes that many modern psychological disorders may be partly rooted in our estrangement from the natural world. From this perspective, reconnection with nature is not just a nice add-on to therapy, but an essential component of psychological well-being.

Psychedelic experiences often dissolve the perceived boundaries between self and world, potentially facilitating a shift from an individualistic, ego-centered consciousness to a more ecological consciousness that recognizes our embeddedness in larger living systems. This shift aligns with what deep ecologists call the "ecological self"—an expanded sense of identity that includes the more-than-human world.

From Experience to Action

While increased nature-connectedness is valuable in itself, the real potential of psychedelics for planetary wellness lies in their ability to inspire and catalyze pro-environmental behaviors and values. Research suggests that nature-connectedness is associated with:

  • Increased pro-environmental attitudes and behaviors
  • Greater willingness to make sacrifices for environmental protection
  • More sustainable consumption patterns
  • Higher levels of environmental concern and engagement

Anecdotally, many report that psychedelic experiences sparked or deepened their environmental commitment, inspiring career changes, lifestyle shifts, and engagement with conservation efforts.

Research and Applications

Emerging research directions in this area include:

  • Environmental Education: Could psychedelic experiences, properly facilitated and integrated, enhance the effectiveness of environmental education programs?
  • Conservation Psychology: Might psychedelic experiences help address the psychological barriers to climate action, such as psychological distance and existential anxiety?
  • Ecological Grief: As climate impacts worsen, could psychedelics play a role in helping process ecological grief and trauma, supporting emotional resilience?
  • Sustainability Leadership: Could carefully guided psychedelic experiences help develop the visionary, systems-thinking leadership needed for planetary healing?

Beyond Romanticism: Practical Considerations

It's important to avoid romanticizing psychedelics as a simple solution to complex ecological challenges. While they may help shift consciousness, addressing the planetary crisis requires comprehensive social, economic, and political transformations. Psychedelics might help inspire and sustain such efforts, but they are not a substitute for concrete action and systemic change.

Similarly, while indigenous traditions offer valuable ecological wisdom, we must be careful not to appropriate these traditions or reduce them to environmental lessons for Western consumption. True respect involves supporting indigenous rights, sovereignty, and leadership in environmental movements.

A Vision of Reciprocity

At its heart, the connection between psychedelics and planetary wellness points toward a vision of reciprocity—a mutual flourishing of human and more-than-human communities. Rather than seeing nature as something to be protected from humans, this perspective recognizes that humans can be active, positive participants in ecological systems when guided by the right relationship.

As author Robin Wall Kimmerer writes, "All flourishing is mutual." Perhaps one of the most profound gifts of psychedelic experiences is the visceral recognition of this truth—that our wellness and the planet's wellness are not separate, but aspects of one interconnected whole.
